Special: Letter from the Editor

The Fire Behind the Eyes

By Cole Hanna

December 7th, 1941

Freedom of Speech by Norman Rockwell

In Matthew 18:20, we are told, “For where two or three are gathered together in my name, there am I in the midst of them.” In Matthew 10:35–37, we are told, “For I have come to set a man against his father, a daughter against her mother, and a daughter-in-law against her mother-in-law; and one’s enemies will be those of his household.” Abraham Lincoln, in an address given at the Illinois State Capitol in 1858, quoted Mark 3:25: “A house divided against itself, cannot stand.”

It is trite and tired to say that, in today’s society, we are farther apart and thinner between each other than almost ever before. What binds us together as a society, in the grandiloquent words of Thomas Jefferson, is that, “We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal.” Just as Frederick Douglass condemned the Fourth of July for its hypocritical celebration as “the mournful wail of millions” cried out, we cannot say that we are living up to the expectations of our forebears: in our lethargy, we have failed to make ambition “counteract ambition” (James Madison, Federalist No. 51). Those who speak the truth—like Jesus Christ, like Socrates—often end up on the wrong side of death’s thin, flowing veil. And today, it is often harsh truths that tear us apart, much like Christ predicted his commandments would.

It is less trite and tired to offer real solutions to these problems; to refer not to the failures of society’s attempts at dialogue, but to envision potential successes. In our first meeting, I asked whether we high school students felt that civil, genuine, and open dialogue was sorely missing in our society: as far as I could see, every hand in that room was raised.

In that first meeting, I saw the great potential, the fire behind the eyes of every student to find out what is right. In debating complex topics, like the limits of the First Amendment and the moral arc of history, students maintained their composure and offered each other grace.

This magazine intends to deliver these conversations to a wider audience; not only to prove that real dialogue is possible, but to show what it looks like. We will cover current events from an ethical perspective, combining sophisticated moral argumentation with rigorous fact-checking; we will discuss moral, ethical, political, and legal topics with a specific focus on presenting diverse viewpoints; we will survey the boundaries of faith and reason, and attempt to find a proper balance between practical and divine truth; and we will interview figures inside and outside our school community to try to find some common ground beneath our slipping feet.

This magazine tries to find that footing together.

If you’d like to discuss contributing or would like information about meeting dates and locations, reach out to Editor-in-Chief and Co-Captain Cole Hanna at cole.hanna@student.camdencatholic.org.
